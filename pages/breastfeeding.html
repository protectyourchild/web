Breastfeeding



Definition


It is the feeding of babies and young children with milk from a woman's breast. According to World Health Organization (WHO) Breastfeeding is one of the most effective ways to ensure child health and survival, Breastmilk is the ideal food for infants. It is safe, clean and contains antibodies which help protect against many common childhood illnesses.



Importance


Breastfeeding is important to keep the baby healthy. It is recommended until babies are around 6 months of age and continued for as long as mother and child desire. Breastmilk provides essential nutrients that the baby needs for their growth and development. It also contains Antibodies that help your baby fight off viruses and bacteria and protects your baby from illness and chronic diseases.



Benefits


for the baby


Breast milk provides necessary nutrients for babies, everything that the baby needs for their growth. It also contains Antibodies that help your baby fight off viruses and bacteria. Breastfeeding lowers your babies’ risk of having asthma or allergies. Also Physical closeness, skin to skin touching and eye contact all help your baby bond with you and feel secure. Plus babies who are breastfed have fewer hospitalizations and trips to doctor.



for the mother


Mothers who breastfeed lowers the risk of breast and ovarian cancer. It also saves the time and money since you don’t have to buy and measure formula, sterilize nipples or warm bottles. It also gives you regular time to relax quietly with your new born as you bond.



Correct Latch-on NAD Positioning


The position in which you hold your baby is also crucial. If your baby is not properly latched on to your breast, feedings could be painful. When a good position and latch is obtained, breastfeeding can be a wonderful experience between mother and baby.



Steps To A Good Latch

  1. Tickle your baby's lips with your nipple. This will help baby open their mouth wide.
  2. Aim your nipple just above your baby's top lip. Make sure your baby's chin isn't tucked into their chest.
  3. Aim your baby's lower lip away from the base of your nipple. Baby's lips should be turned outward like a fish. Your baby should lead into the breast chin first and then latch onto your breast. Your baby's tongue should be extended, and your breast should fill your baby's mouth.
  4. If your baby latches just on the tip of your nipple or it hurts, gently put a clean finger in your baby's mouth to break the latch, then try again.


Positions for Breastfeeding




Side-lying-hold

Lets you rest and relax while your baby feeds. Lie down, with your body and your baby's body parallel. Snuggle up to your baby and bring her to your breast.





Cradle hold (same arm)

Get comfy in a chair or on the sofa. Put a pillow or cushion on your lap to support your baby. This will make feeding easier on your arms.

Support your baby's head with the opposite arm to the breast you are feeding from. Gently bring your baby's head to your breast.





Cradle hold (opposite arm)

Lay a pillow or cushion on your lap. Place your baby on the pillow, supporting her body with the opposite arm to your breast. Your baby's head can rest snugly in the crook of your arm while she feeds.





Rugby ball hold

If your baby is a kicker, this may be the position for you. Place your baby so that her body is underneath one of your arms, with her head near your breast. Support her head with the same arm as your breast.





Twins hold

Make yourself comfortable, and get a large pillow or cushion to put on your lap. This will help you to support both of your babies. If possible, have someone nearby who can pass you your babies.

Try putting one baby under each arm, with their heads near your breasts. You can use the same arm as your breast to support each baby.





Koala hold

Support your baby while she's straddled across your knees, well-supported, in an upright position.





Laid-back breastfeeding (biological nurturing)

Lie down in a semi-reclined position. Encourage your baby to lie across your stomach or shoulder and find her own way to your breast.





Laid-back breastfeeding after a caesarean

Lie down in a semi-reclined position, with your baby lying vertically on you. This will help to avoid her touching your healing wound as she finds your breast to feed.
